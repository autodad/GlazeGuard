The Power of Ice: Water expansion can exert pressures exceeding 200 MPa (29,000 psi). This is more than enough to actuate a mechanical stud, even under the weight of a 140kg person. The challenge isn't force, it's containment and speed.

The Role of Nanocellulose in the PCPT-Cell

In a standard "water-only" cell, ice crystals can form in unpredictable patterns, and pure ice is notoriously brittle. By mixing in a small percentage of nanocellulose, we create a reinforced ice-composite.


1. Controlled Crystallization (Nucleation)
Pure distilled water can sometimes "supercool" (stay liquid below 0°C) if it’s too still. Nanocellulose acts as a nucleating agent, providing surfaces for ice crystals to grab onto. This ensures the "Ice-Drive" activates immediately at the freezing point.


2. Structural Toughness (The "Pykrete" Effect)
During World War II, there was a project called Pykrete (wood pulp + ice) used to make ice as strong as concrete. By using nanocellulose, we create a modern version:

    The Benefit: It prevents the ice core from shattering under the sudden impact of a 140kg person jumping. The fibers distribute the mechanical stress throughout the ice-core.

3. Thermal Conductivity
Nanocellulose can help speed up the freezing/thawing process by slightly altering the thermal properties of the mixture, making the shoe respond faster when you step outside.


The Reinforced Ice-Composite (NC-Ice)
To ensure reliability, the PCPT-cell uses a mixture of distilled water and 1-3% Nanocellulose (CNC/CNF). This creates a "Micro-Pykrete" effect, offering three distinct advantages:

    Nucleation: Prevents supercooling, ensuring rapid activation at 0°C.

    Shatter-Resistance: The cellulose fibers reinforce the ice matrix, allowing it to withstand high-impact loads from heavy users (up to 140kg).

    Consistent Expansion: It ensures the 9% expansion force is distributed evenly against the bistable membrane.
